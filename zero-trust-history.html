Zero Trust: From Network Perimeters to a Security Mindset Zero Trust is a term I hear almost daily. Sometimes it’s used to describe a product, sometimes a framework, and sometimes just “MFA everywhere”. In my experience, most disagreements about Zero Trust come from one simple issue: people confuse controls with mindset. This post is my take on where Zero Trust came from, why it emerged, and how it has evolved into the security approach I use today. Life behind the perimeter Early in my career, security architectures were built around a very clear assumption: If you are inside the network, you are trusted. Firewalls, VPNs, and network segmentation did the heavy lifting. Once users or systems were “in”, they usually had broad access. Security was primarily about preventing initial access. At the time, that approach made sense: applications lived in datacenters users were on corporate networks devices were owned and managed by IT The problem was not that the model was wrong — it was that the world changed. When implicit trust becomes the problem As cloud services, SaaS platforms, and remote work became the norm, the perimeter started to dissolve. What I repeatedly saw in incidents and breach reports followed the same pattern: stolen credentials initial access that looked legitimate lateral movement with very few obstacles impact far beyond the original compromise The painful lesson was consistent: trust based on location scales very badly. Once attackers were “inside”, the architecture often helped them rather than slowed them down. The idea behind Zero Trust Zero Trust flips the old assumption on its head: Never trust implicitly. Always verify. Instead of asking “Where is the request coming from?”, the focus shifts to: who is making the request from what device to which resource under what conditions and with what level of risk In my view, this is where Zero Trust becomes valuable — not as a checklist, but as a way to reason about access. Not a product, not a destination One of the biggest mistakes I still see is treating Zero Trust as something you can “implement and be done with”. Zero Trust is: not a single tool not a finished state not something you buy once It is an architectural approach that influences how systems are designed, integrated, and operated over time. The principles are fairly stable: continuous verification least privilege assume breach reduce blast radius visibility everywhere How those principles are applied will always depend on the organization, maturity level, and risk appetite. How Zero Trust evolved in practice Over time, Zero Trust grew beyond identity alone. In environments I’ve worked with, a practical Zero Trust architecture usually spans: Identity – authentication, access decisions, and governance Devices – health, compliance, and workload identity Applications – exposure management and access boundaries Data – classification, protection, and policy enforcement Infrastructure – segmentation, monitoring, and continuous assessment The most important shift is not technical. It is the move from one-time decisions to continuous evaluation. Final thoughts Zero Trust did not emerge because it was trendy. It emerged because older trust models failed under modern conditions. In my experience, organizations that succeed with Zero Trust accept that: it is a journey, not a milestone architecture matters more than tooling identity and visibility are foundational At its core, Zero Trust is about removing blind trust from systems that are too complex to fully control.
